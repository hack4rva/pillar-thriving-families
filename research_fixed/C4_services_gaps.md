
# Closing the Gaps in Richmond: Youth Jobs Access and Maternal Data Coordination

## Executive Summary

Richmond can close its most critical service gaps by doubling paid youth placements, fixing documentation and transportation barriers, and standing up a maternal data collaborative that turns fragmented data into coordinated postpartum care. While the city has made strides in both youth workforce development and maternal health, capacity constraints and coordination bottlenecks prevent these systems from meeting full community demand. For youth, demand vastly outstrips program capacity, particularly for older, out-of-school young adults. For maternal health, the most significant risks occur in the postpartum period, where data disconnects between hospitals and community-based organizations (CBOs) hinder timely interventions. 

## Youth Workforce Gaps

Program slots and employer hosts lag behind demand, especially for 16–24-year-olds. Practical fixes require employer recruitment, wage subsidies, transport and ID clinics, and a unified application portal.

### Demand vs. Capacity

Documented participation and statewide out-of-school time (OST) waitlists indicate significant unmet demand for Richmond youth employment. The Office of Community Wealth Building's (OCWB) Youth Works RVA program served 703 youth participants in FY2025 (603 during the summer program and 100 in other tracks) [1]. These youth worked at 114 partner employers and earned $1,106,225 in wages [1]. However, broader data suggests a massive unmet need: for every child in an afterschool program in Virginia, four are waiting to get in [2]. 

| Metric | Current Supply (FY2025) | Inferred Demand Indicator | Gap Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Total Youth Served | 703 participants [1] | 4:1 statewide OST waitlist ratio [2] | Thousands of youth likely lack access to summer employment. |
| Employer Hosts | 114 partner employers [1] | 65% of employers would hire interns if positions were available [1] | Employer willingness exists, but funded positions or matching mechanisms are insufficient. |

### Where Youth Fall Through the Cracks

Older "opportunity youth" and younger teens without documents or transportation face the steepest barriers. The YES Forward program specifically aims to support older youth, aged 18 to 24, who are out of school or unemployed [3]. However, *Inference: Limited published throughput data for YES Forward suggests a capacity gap in serving this specific demographic at scale.* Additionally, basic documentation is a hurdle; the Richmond Redevelopment & Housing Authority has had to host specific events to help youth get their documents in order for summer applications [4]. 

### Geographic Access Gaps

Middle schools have systematized OST with transport, but high school and summer jobs depend on where employers are, creating uneven access. The NextUp Middle School Alliance provides free afterschool programs at all seven Richmond Public Middle Schools, complete with food and transportation home [5]. Conversely, summer worksites cluster where hosts exist. *Inference: Program deserts likely exist in the Southside and East End due to employer clustering, though no public map currently tracks high-school or summer worksite placement density.*

### Gap Inventory by Type

Capacity limits (slots and hosts) are the primary constraints, while access barriers (transport, IDs) and information fragmentation compound the gap.

### 12-Month Action Plan

A focused package can lift placements significantly in a year. Actions include launching an employer host drive to add 150 sites, distributing 400 youth transit passes, hosting 6 mobile document clinics, and launching a 200-seat YES Forward cohort for 18-24 year olds.

## Maternal Health Data & Care Coordination Gaps

Most preventable harm occurs postpartum and in substance use disorder (SUD) contexts. The Emergency Department Care Coordination (EDCC) program is promising but not yet universal.

### Coordination Breakpoints

Late postpartum deaths and overdose patterns highlight missing handoffs between OB, primary care, and behavioral health. Between 2018 and 2022, 37.4% of pregnancy-associated deaths occurred between 43-365 days after the end of pregnancy, and 40% were due to accidental overdose [6]. To address this, Virginia Health Information's (VHI) EDCC program now allows providers to set up alerts for patients presenting in hospitals with a recent history of delivery, and includes a Substance Exposed Infant (SEI) flag [7]. 

### Data Producer-User Disconnects

State and hospital systems produce data, but CBOs and local coalitions need analytic capacity to act quickly. The Virginia Department of Health (VDH) provides robust dashboards using data from birth certificates, hospitalizations, and PRAMS [6]. However, *Inference: Smaller CBOs and doula collectives often lack dedicated data analysts to process this information.* Organizations like Urban Baby Beginnings demonstrate the power of bridging this gap; they use the EDCC program to identify when a client receives emergency care and follow up quickly [8].

### Indicator Inconsistencies That Confuse Decisions

Different definitions and geographies reduce comparability and mask inequities. VDH dashboards track indicators like late or no prenatal care, low birthweight, and preterm birth [9]. However, *Inference: Local reports often mix sources (e.g., ZIP code vs. census tract, varying definitions of prenatal care adequacy), leading to misalignment in community health assessments.*

### Gap Inventory by Type

Capacity (analytic and care coordination) is the binding constraint in maternal health.

| Gap Type | Breakpoint | Example | Evidence | Recommended Fix |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Capacity** | Postpartum / SUD | CBOs lack data analysts | Urban Baby Beginnings relies on EDCC [8] | Shared fractional data analyst for CBOs |
| **Information** | Prenatal / Postpartum | Inconsistent indicator definitions | VDH dashboard method notes [6] | Richmond Maternal Metrics Playbook |
| **Access** | Postpartum | ED-to-outpatient handoffs | 37.4% of deaths occur 43-365 days postpartum [6] | Universal EDCC maternal alerting |

### Build the Maternal Data Collaborative

A lightweight governance model with EDCC onboarding and a shared dataset will translate data into coordinated care. RHHD should convene hospitals, FQHCs, and CBOs to establish a data-sharing MOU and provide technical assistance on using EDCC and VDH dashboards.

## Cross-Cutting Moves

The same enablers—navigators, transport, document clinics, and shared analytics—unlock impact across youth employment and maternal care.

### Joint Infrastructure Plays

One citywide navigator network and analytics hub can support both domains efficiently. Co-located clinics can provide IDs for youth while connecting mothers to benefits and care referrals.

### Funding and Policy Levers

Combine WIOA Out-of-School Youth funds, city continuation funds, Medicaid SUD/postpartum benefits, and VHI/EDCC support to sustain scale.

### Risks and Mitigations

Minimize harm with strong governance and equity metrics. Risks include data sharing compliance and uneven neighborhood reach. Mitigations require de-identified analytics, opt-in consents, and multi-year MOUs.
