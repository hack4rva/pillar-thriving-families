# Global Youth Employment Outlook 2024-25: Unequal Recovery, Rising NEET Risks, and Policy Levers

## Executive Summary

The global youth labor market is experiencing a fragmented recovery. As of 2023, the global youth unemployment rate dropped to 13%, representing 64.9 million unemployed youth—the lowest level in 15 years [1]. However, this headline figure masks deep structural vulnerabilities and widening inequalities. While the employment-to-population ratio (EPR) rebounded to 35%, the gender gap remains stark, with young women facing a double disadvantage in both job losses and recovery rates [1]. 

Crucially, unemployment figures do not capture the full extent of youth labor market exclusion. In 2023, 20.4% of the global youth population (256 million) were Not in Employment, Education, or Training (NEET) [2]. The International Labour Organization (ILO) projects this number will rise to 262 million—or one in four youth—by 2025 [3]. Regional disparities are severe: Sub-Saharan Africa is expected to generate three-quarters of global youth employment gains through 2025, yet its youth labor force will expand by 72.6 million by 2050, threatening mass underemployment [2]. Conversely, regions like the Arab States and North Africa struggle with NEET rates exceeding 30%, predominantly affecting young women [2]. To prevent a lost generation, policymakers must urgently pivot from broad economic recovery tactics to targeted, gender-responsive job creation and education-labor alignment strategies.

## Global Landscape Overview

The post-pandemic recovery has brought the global youth unemployment rate down to 13% in 2023, a 15-year low, leaving 64.9 million youth unemployed [1]. Despite this positive macroeconomic signal, the underlying dynamics reveal persistent fragility.

### Unemployment Rate Trends (2000-2023)

The 2023 unemployment rate of 13% is 0.8 percentage points below the pre-pandemic rate of 13.8% in 2019, and significantly lower than the crisis peak of 15.6% in 2020 [2]. However, the recovery has been unequal across genders. The youth unemployment rate dropped more sharply for young men than for young women during the recovery period, shifting the gender dynamics of employment transitions and placing a greater degree of disadvantage on young women [2]. The global youth unemployment rate is projected to decrease slightly to 12.8% by 2025 [2].

### Employment-to-Population Ratio (EPR) Dynamics

By 2023, youth employment regained its COVID-19 losses, reaching 435 million employed young people globally—a number not seen since 2016 [2]. The global youth EPR rebounded to 35% [2]. However, the gender gap in the youth EPR remains deeply entrenched. In 2023, the male-to-female gap stood at 13.1 percentage points [2]. The ILO notes that if the current pace of change continues (narrowing by only one percentage point per decade), the gender gap in youth EPR will not close until well into the twenty-second century [2].

### NEET Share and Projections

The NEET rate provides a more accurate indicator of the barriers young people face in entering the labor market [3]. In 2023, 256.3 million young people (20.4% of the youth cohort) were NEET [2]. This includes 171 million young women and 85 million young men, meaning two in three young NEETs globally are women [2] [5]. The ILO estimates that by 2025, the number of NEET youth will climb to 261.9 million, maintaining the 20.4% global rate [2]. 

## Regional Deep-Dive

The global averages obscure vastly different regional realities, driven by demographic shifts and localized economic constraints.

### Sub-Saharan Africa – Growth Engine & Vulnerability

Sub-Saharan Africa is the only region to see sizable growth in youth employment over the last two decades [2]. It is projected to account for three-quarters of the global youth employment gains through 2025 [2]. However, this job creation is outpaced by explosive demographic growth. The youth labor force in Africa is projected to grow by 76 million by 2050 [5]. Without massive investments in formal job creation, this demographic dividend risks translating into widespread informal underemployment.

### Arab States & North Africa – Highest NEET Rates

The Arab States and North Africa face the most severe youth labor market exclusion globally. In 2023, the Arab States recorded a youth NEET rate of 33.2%, while North Africa stood at 31.2% [2]. Furthermore, one in three youth in these regions are unemployed [5]. These high rates are heavily gendered, reflecting systemic barriers to young women's participation in both education and the workforce.

### East Asia & Eastern Europe – Rising NEET & Declining Labor Force

In contrast to Africa, East Asia and Eastern Europe are facing demographic contractions. East Asia's youth labor force is projected to shrink by 32.7 million between 2023 and 2050 [2]. Despite these shrinking cohorts, youth NEET rates are expected to increase in East Asia and Eastern Europe through 2025 [2], signaling a growing mismatch between educational outputs and labor market demands.

### High-Income Economies (North America, Europe) – Stagnating/Increasing Unemployment

While high-income countries generally maintain lower NEET rates (below 15%), they are not immune to negative trends [2]. North America and Northern, Southern, and Western Europe are the only two subregions expected to experience an increase in their youth unemployment rates through 2025 [2]. North America's rate is projected to rise from 8.2% in 2023 to 8.4% in 2025 [2].

## Structural Challenges

### Skills-Job Mismatch

Educational attainment is increasingly failing to guarantee secure employment. Two in three young adult workers in developing economies hold qualifications that do not match well to their jobs [5]. This educational mismatch traps young people in low-productivity roles and stifles broader economic innovation.

### Informal & Insecure Employment

The lack of decent jobs remains a pervasive issue. In low-income countries, only 1 in 5 young adult workers (aged 25-29) are in a regular paid job, compared to 4 in 5 in high-income countries [5]. In Sub-Saharan Africa, 3 in 4 youth lack secure work [5]. This reliance on informal, temporary, or own-account work leaves youth highly vulnerable to economic shocks.

### Data Limitations & Monitoring Needs

Effective policy is hindered by data gaps. The ILO notes that 33% of the world's youth live in a country that is "off track" in its progress to meet SDG target 8.6 (reducing NEET rates), with regressive trends concentrated in low-income countries where data collection is often weakest [2].

## Policy Recommendations

### Job-Creation & Green/Digital Economy Strategies
Governments must move beyond generic growth policies and target sectors with high youth-absorption capacity. Investments in the green economy, digital infrastructure, and care sectors should be paired with hiring subsidies for youth to ensure that macroeconomic growth translates into youth employment.

### Gender-Responsive Interventions
With two-thirds of young NEETs being women [5], policies must address the specific barriers keeping women out of the workforce. This includes state-subsidized childcare, safe transport infrastructure, and targeted apprenticeship programs designed to transition young women from unpaid care work into formal employment.

### Education-Labor Market Alignment
To combat the reality that two-thirds of youth in developing economies face educational mismatch [5], education systems must be tightly integrated with private sector needs. Employer-led curriculum co-design, vocational training expansion, and credential-stacking frameworks are essential to make graduates employable.

### Strengthening Data Systems
Governments and international donors must invest in robust, high-frequency labor market data collection. Tracking NEET rates disaggregated by gender, region, and disability status is critical for deploying active labor market policies (ALMPs) effectively.

## Risks & Contingency Scenarios

The projected "soft landing" of the global economy [2] remains vulnerable to geopolitical shocks and inflation. If global growth stalls, the fragile gains in youth employment will evaporate, as youth are typically the first to be laid off. Furthermore, rising youth anxiety is palpable: 2 in 3 young people are worried about losing their jobs [5]. Failure to provide secure employment pathways risks severe social unrest, particularly in regions like Sub-Saharan Africa where the youth population is surging.
