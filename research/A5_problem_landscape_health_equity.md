# A5 Problem Landscape — Health Equity (2026)

*Prepared on 2026-03-24*

## Executive Summary

Health equity in the United States remains uneven despite modest gains in life expectancy and declines in overall mortality. Core indicators show persistent gaps by race, ethnicity, and income, while emerging policy frameworks—such as the CMS Framework for Health Equity 2022–2032 [1] —and data-driven tools offer pathways to accelerate progress. This document synthesizes the latest national statistics, policy developments, and scholarly insights to provide a clear problem-landscape for decision-makers working to close health-outcome gaps.

## Context & Definitions

**Health equity** is defined as "the state in which everyone has a fair and just opportunity to attain their highest level of health" [2]. 
**Problem landscape** refers to a systematic mapping of key metrics, drivers, and policy levers that shape equity outcomes across the U.S. health system.

## Core Health-Equity Metrics (2022–2024)

Recent data from the National Center for Health Statistics and other federal tracking systems reveal a complex picture of recovery and persistent disparity. Overall health outcomes are improving, yet the magnitude of benefit is disproportionately captured by advantaged groups. Racial and ethnic gaps in maternal and infant mortality remain stark, and the uninsured burden persists for low-income populations.

| Indicator | 2022/2023 Value | Disparity Highlights |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Life expectancy at birth (total population)** | 77.5 years in 2022 (an increase of 1.1 years from 2021) [3] | For males, life expectancy increased 1.3 years to 74.8; for females, it increased 0.9 years to 80.2 [3]. |
| **Uninsured rate (age 0–64)** | 9.5% (25.3 million people) in 2023 [4] [5] | The uninsured rate remained statistically unchanged from the previous year, highlighting a persistent coverage gap for millions of nonelderly residents [5]. |
| **Maternal mortality (deaths per 100k live births)** | 16.9 overall in 2022 [6] | Non-Hispanic Black women experienced a rate of 69.9, compared to 13.2 for Non-Hispanic White women and 16.9 for Hispanic women [6]. |
| **Infant mortality (deaths per 100k live births)** | 560.4 in 2022 (an increase of 3.1% from 543.6 in 2021) [3] | Significant increases were noted in maternal complications and bacterial sepsis of the newborn [3]. |
| **Age-adjusted death rate** | 798.8 per 100k in 2022 (a decrease of 9.2% from 2021) [3] | Age-adjusted death rates decreased for all race and Hispanic-origin groups for both males and females [3]. |

*Key Takeaway:* While top-line mortality and life expectancy metrics showed improvement in 2022 largely due to a 55.3% decrease in COVID-19 deaths [3], severe inequities remain embedded in maternal health and chronic disease outcomes.

## Primary Drivers of Persistent Inequities

Addressing any single metric will have limited effect unless the upstream socio-economic, data, and policy structures are simultaneously tackled.

| Driver | Evidence & Impact | Policy Levers |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Socio-economic inequality** | Higher income is associated with greater longevity throughout the income distribution. The gap in life expectancy between the richest 1% and poorest 1% is 14.6 years for men and 10.1 years for women [7]. | Targeted income supports, community investment, and housing policies. |
| **Insurance coverage gaps** | 25.3 million nonelderly individuals remained uninsured in 2023 [4]. Lack of insurance delays diagnosis and limits chronic disease management. | Medicaid expansion in remaining states; enhanced subsidies for ACA marketplace enrollment. |
| **Structural racism in data collection** | Misclassification of race and ethnicity on death certificates underestimates American Indian and Alaska Native (AIAN) mortality by 34%, and Hispanic/Asian mortality by 3% [3]. | Standardized race/ethnicity reporting; investment in community-based registries and data validation. |
| **Digital health divide** | Poorly designed digital health interventions (DHIs) can precipitate equity-related harms and biases if not rigorously tested [8]. | Mandate equity assessments and human assessment rubrics in DHIs [8]. |
| **Policy fragmentation** | The CMS Framework for Health Equity provides a strong foundation [1], but inconsistent state-level implementation leads to uneven progress across jurisdictions. | Federal-state coordination mechanisms; performance-based funding tied to equity metrics. |

*Key Takeaway:* Inequities are compounded by systemic data flaws and socio-economic divides. Accurate data collection and inclusive design are prerequisites for effective policy intervention.

## Emerging Opportunities

1. **CMS Health-Equity Framework (2022–2032):** This framework provides a comprehensive roadmap for integrating equity into payment, quality measurement, and community health initiatives, serving as a trusted partner dedicated to advancing health equity [1].
2. **National Healthcare Quality & Disparities Report (NHQDR 2023):** The AHRQ report assesses the performance of the healthcare system and identifies specific areas of strengths and weaknesses regarding access and quality, offering a vital baseline for benchmarking [9].
3. **Life-Expectancy Income Research:** Studies utilizing deidentified tax records demonstrate that differences in life expectancy across income groups vary substantially across local areas, correlating strongly with health behaviors and local area characteristics [7]. This suggests that place-based interventions can successfully narrow gaps.
4. **Equity-by-Design Toolkits:** New methodologies and human assessment rubrics are being developed to surface biases and equity-related harms in medical AI and digital health tools, ensuring that technological advancements do not leave vulnerable populations behind [8].

## Key Failure Cases & Cautionary Lessons

| Failure Case | What Went Wrong | Lesson Learned |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Data Misclassification** | Inconsistencies in reporting Hispanic origin and race on death certificates led to a 34% underestimation of death rates for the AIAN population [3]. | Relying on unadjusted data masks severe crises in marginalized communities. Data must be adjusted for misclassification to reflect true mortality burdens [3]. |
| **Uneven Geographic Outcomes** | Between 2001 and 2014, life expectancy changes for the bottom income quartile ranged from gains of more than 4 years in some areas to losses of more than 2 years in others [7]. | National policies must account for local area characteristics; a one-size-fits-all approach fails to address localized social determinants of health. |
| **Unchecked AI in Healthcare** | Long-form, LLM-generated answers to medical queries carry the potential to precipitate equity-related harms if biases are not surfaced [8]. | Digital health tools require iterative, participatory approaches to design and rigorous bias testing before deployment [8]. |

*Key Takeaway:* Well-intentioned programs frequently fail when they ignore local contexts, deploy untested technologies, or rely on flawed demographic data.

## Strategic Recommendations

1. **Integrate Equity KPIs into All Health Programs:** Align federal and state incentives using the priorities outlined in the CMS Framework for Health Equity [1]. 
2. **Mandate Standardized Race/Ethnicity Reporting:** Implement strict data validation protocols to correct the misclassification of AIAN, Hispanic, and Asian populations on vital records, ensuring resources are allocated based on accurate mortality burdens [3].
3. **Scale Community-Led Digital Health Co-Design:** Require developers of digital health interventions and medical LLMs to utilize established toolboxes for surfacing health equity harms and biases prior to clinical deployment [8].
4. **Target Localized Social Determinants:** Because life expectancy for low-income individuals varies by up to 4.5 years depending on geographic location [7], funding should be directed toward local public health initiatives that address specific community health behaviors and environmental factors.

## Closing Remarks

The U.S. health-equity landscape is at a pivotal juncture. While life expectancy and overall mortality showed signs of recovery in 2022, entrenched socioeconomic and structural barriers keep vulnerable groups—particularly Black and Indigenous populations—far behind. By embedding equity into policy design, enforcing accurate data collection, and rigorously testing new health technologies for bias, stakeholders can transform modest national gains into systemic, lasting progress for all communities.
