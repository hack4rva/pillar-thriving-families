# Richmond's Maternal Health Equity Gaps: Where to Aim the Next Dollar

## Executive Summary

Richmond faces a severe maternal and infant health crisis characterized by stark racial disparities and geographic concentration. Richmond City's preterm birth rate sits at an alarming **12.5%** (an "F" grade from the March of Dimes), significantly higher than both state and national averages [1]. The burden of poor outcomes falls disproportionately on Black families: in Virginia, the Black infant mortality rate is **2.2 times higher** than the White rate [2], mirroring national trends where Black maternal mortality is nearly **3.5 times higher** than White maternal mortality [3]. 

Furthermore, the crisis extends beyond the delivery room. In Virginia, **40%** of pregnancy-associated deaths are due to accidental overdoses, with **37.4%** occurring between 43 and 365 days postpartum [4]. To drive meaningful change, stakeholders must focus resources on high-burden neighborhoods in the East End and Southside, leveraging recent policy wins like Virginia's 12-month Medicaid postpartum extension and community doula benefits [5]. 

## Purpose and Decision Frame

Focus the tool on the few indicators that move preterm birth, infant mortality, and maternal harm—by race and neighborhood—so funders and agencies can target interventions with precision.

### Stakeholder Prioritization, Not Patient Triage
This research supports the architecture of a strategic data dashboard designed for public health officials, hospital administrators, and community-based organizations. It is not a clinical triage tool. The objective is to illuminate the scale and shape of maternal health inequities in Richmond so that stakeholders can allocate funding, place physical resources, and measure the impact of policy interventions (like Medicaid expansion and doula coverage) at the population level.

### Defining Equity Success
Success is defined by shrinking the Black-White gap in maternal and infant outcomes while simultaneously improving overall baseline rates. A dashboard must make these disparity ratios visible, holding programs accountable to narrowing the gaps year over year rather than just moving the citywide average.

## Current Outcomes vs. Benchmarks

Richmond's preterm rate is an outlier; Virginia's infant and maternal mortality remain above national targets, with stark racial gaps.

### Side-by-Side Metrics That Matter Now

| Metric | Richmond (City) | Virginia | United States | Source |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Preterm Birth Rate** | 12.5% (Grade F) | 10.1% (Grade C-) | 10.4% | March of Dimes 2025 [1] |
| **Infant Mortality Rate** | N/A (City-level suppressed) | 5.9 per 1,000 births | 5.61 per 1,000 births | March of Dimes 2025 [1]; CDC NCHS 2023 [6] |
| **Maternal Mortality Rate** | N/A (City-level suppressed) | 33.4 per 100,000 births | 22.3 per 100,000 births | March of Dimes 2025 [1]; CDC NCHS 2022 [7] |

*Takeaway*: Richmond City is failing in preterm birth prevention, lagging significantly behind the state and the nation. Virginia as a whole struggles with maternal mortality, posting rates well above the national average.

### Interpretation and Caveats
When building the dashboard, note that maternal mortality rates fluctuate year-to-year due to small sample sizes and reporting delays [8] [7]. City-level maternal and infant mortality data are frequently suppressed if the count is lower than 20 to protect privacy and ensure statistical reliability [8]. Therefore, 3-year to 5-year rolling averages must be used for local mortality metrics.

## Racial Inequities

The Black-White gap in infant and maternal outcomes is large and persistent; the dashboard must make disparity closure visible and actionable.

### Evidence Snapshot: The Black-White Divide

| **Infant Mortality Rate** | 2.2x higher than White | Baseline | Virginia (2021) | VDH Annual Report [2] |

*Takeaway*: The racial disparities are extreme and systemic. In the U.S., the maternal mortality rate for Black women is significantly higher than for White, Hispanic, and Asian women [3]. 

### Dashboard Actions
To operationalize this data, the dashboard must include disparity ratios (e.g., Black:White IMR and preterm ratios) for each outcome and process metric. Program funding and interventions should be tied directly to milestones that close these specific gaps.

## Geography of Burden

Use the Richmond and Henrico Health District (RHHD) service footprint and the citywide "F" preterm grade to prioritize East End and Southside ZIP codes.

### Known Signals and Historical Context
While exact ZIP-level mortality rates are often suppressed, the physical placement of RHHD Resource Centers provides a clear map of community need. RHHD operates centers heavily concentrated in the East End (Creighton, Gilpin, Fairfield, Mosby, Whitcomb) and the Southside (Hillside, Southwood) [9]. Furthermore, historical redlining continues to impact these areas. For example, the Gilpin neighborhood faces high pollution, few trees, and high temperatures, leading to significantly worse health conditions [10]. High-risk ZIP codes identified in the region include **23223** (East End) and **23224** (Southside) [11].

### Implementation Strategy
The dashboard should build 3-year rolling rates at the ZIP or census-tract level for higher-volume indicators like preterm birth, low birthweight, and late/no prenatal care. It should overlay the locations of WIC offices, doula services, and maternity navigation centers to monitor whether resources are reaching the hottest spots.

## Contributing Factors and Leverage Points

Address postpartum overdose, inadequate prenatal care, and upstream behavioral risks to bend the preterm and mortality curves.

### Postpartum Overdose and Timing
Maternal health does not end at delivery. In Virginia, between 2018 and 2022, there were **62.3 pregnancy-associated deaths per 100,000 live births** [4]. Strikingly, **40%** of these deaths were due to accidental overdose, and **37.4%** occurred between 43 and 365 days after the end of pregnancy [4]. 
*Action*: The dashboard must track Substance Use Disorder (SUD) screenings, Medication for Opioid Use Disorder (MOUD) initiation, and postpartum visit adherence up to one year.

### Prenatal Care Adequacy
Only **79.5%** of Virginia births received adequate prenatal care in 2024, leaving 1 in 5 births underserved [1]. Inadequate prenatal care rates are notably higher for Black and Pacific Islander mothers [1]. 
*Action*: Track "time-to-first-visit" and "late/no prenatal care" as leading process KPIs.

### Upstream Risks and Chronic Disease
Upstream factors heavily influence Richmond's outcomes. According to 2022 PRAMS data for the Richmond City Health District, **45.07%** of pregnancies were unintended, and **22.42%** were mistimed [12]. Additionally, chronic diseases like asthma and arthritis are highly prevalent in Richmond, complicating pregnancies [13].

## Access and Insurance

Virginia's 12-month postpartum Medicaid extension and doula coverage are massive policy levers—measure whether they reach Black families in high-need ZIPs.

### Policy Wins to Track
Virginia Medicaid covers approximately one in three births [5]. Recent policy changes have created new opportunities for intervention:
* **12-Month Postpartum Coverage**: In 2021, Virginia received federal approval to extend Medicaid postpartum coverage from 60 days to 12 months [5].
* **Community Doula Benefit**: Virginia implemented a state-certified community doula benefit in Medicaid in spring 2022 to combat maternal morbidity and reduce cesarean sections [5].

The dashboard should track births by payer (Medicaid %), coverage continuity (churn rates during redeterminations), and the uptake of doula services.

## Actors and Initiatives

Align dashboard metrics with local organizations doing the groundwork.

* **Richmond and Henrico Health Districts (RHHD)**: Operates a Maternity Navigation line to connect uninsured pregnant people to care, manages WIC offices, and runs community resource centers [14] [9].
* **Urban Baby Beginnings & Birth in Color RVA**: Partnering with state agencies to build sustainable community-based doula programs and safer childbirth cities [5].
* **Virginia Neonatal Perinatal Collaborative (VNPC) & March of Dimes**: Driving quality improvement and highlighting racial disparities at the state and regional levels [5].

## Dashboard Blueprint: Key Indicators to Prioritize

Start with a core set of indicators tied to outcomes and equity, updated quarterly, using consistent definitions.

### Core Indicator Set
1. **Outcomes**: Preterm birth (%), Low birthweight (%), Infant mortality (3-year rolling), Maternal mortality (5-year rolling), Severe maternal morbidity (rate per 10,000 deliveries).
2. **Access/Process**: Late/no prenatal care (%), Adequate prenatal care (%), Time to first prenatal visit, Postpartum visit adherence (7-84 days), Postpartum Medicaid coverage continuity (%), Doula support at delivery (%).
3. **Risk/Behavioral**: SUD screening (%), MOUD initiation (%), Postpartum contraception within 60 days (%), Interpregnancy interval <18 months (%).

### Measurement Standards and Data Governance
* Use 3-year rolling averages for Infant Mortality and 5-year rolling averages for Maternal Mortality to stabilize small numbers.
* Suppress data cells where n < 20 to protect privacy and prevent statistical noise [8].
* Stratify every KPI by race/ethnicity, payer, and ZIP/tract.

## Output Summary

### Facts (with source titles)
* Richmond City's preterm birth rate is 12.5% (Grade F) (*2025 March Of Dimes Report Card For Virginia*) [1].
* Virginia's Black infant mortality rate was 2.2 times higher than the White rate in 2021 (*Virginia Department of Health Annual Report*) [2].
* Nationally, the maternal mortality rate for Black women in 2023 was 50.3 per 100,000, compared to 14.5 for White women (*Maternal Mortality Rates in the United States, 2023*) [3].
* In Virginia, 40% of pregnancy-associated deaths (2018-2022) were due to accidental overdose (*Maternal & Child Health - Data*) [4].
* 37.4% of Virginia's pregnancy-associated deaths occurred between 43 and 365 days after the end of pregnancy (*Maternal & Child Health - Data*) [4].
* 45.07% of pregnancies in the Richmond City Health District were reported as unintended in 2022 (*RCHD, VA PRAMS FACTS- 2022*) [12].
* Virginia Medicaid extended postpartum coverage to 12 months and launched a community doula benefit in 2022 (*Maternal Health Annual Report 2021 - Virginia Medicaid*) [5].

### Inferences (Clearly Labeled)
* *Inference*: Because RHHD has concentrated its physical Resource Centers in East End (Creighton, Gilpin, Fairfield, Mosby) and Southside (Hillside, Southwood) neighborhoods, these specific geographic areas likely face the highest burden of maternal and infant health disparities in the city.
* *Inference*: Given that 65% of all U.S. infant deaths occur among preterm births [6], Richmond's exceptionally high preterm birth rate (12.5%) is the primary driver of its local infant mortality crisis.

### Unknowns (What cannot be verified publicly)
* Exact, single-year maternal mortality rates specifically for Richmond City (data is suppressed due to counts being lower than 20).
* Exact, real-time infant mortality rates broken down simultaneously by both race *and* specific Richmond ZIP codes (requires custom data requests from VDH Vital Records).
* The exact percentage of Richmond City births utilizing the new Medicaid doula benefit since its 2022 implementation.

### Key Indicators that a Shared Dashboard Should Prioritize
1. **Preterm Birth Rate** (Disaggregated by race and ZIP code).
2. **Disparity Ratios** (Black:White ratios for Infant Mortality and Preterm Birth).
3. **Late/No Prenatal Care & Prenatal Care Adequacy** (Leading indicators for birth outcomes).
4. **Postpartum SUD Metrics** (Overdose tracking, MOUD initiation, and 43-365 day postpartum visit adherence).
5. **Medicaid Coverage Continuity & Doula Utilization** (Tracking the reach of new state policy levers).
