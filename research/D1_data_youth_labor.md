# Virginia Teen Work Compliance 2026: Permits, Hours, Hazards, and Curfew—Ages 14–18

## Executive Summary

Navigating Virginia's youth labor laws requires employers and teens to understand a strict framework of permits, hour caps, and safety prohibitions. The rules shift significantly depending on the minor's exact age, the time of year, and the specific tasks they are asked to perform. 

**Key Strategic Insights:**
* **Work permits are the gatekeeper for 14–15-year-olds:** All 14- and 15-year-olds must have an Employment Certificate issued by the Department of Labor and Industry (DOLI) before starting any job [1]. The Virginia Electronic Employment Certificate System (VAeECS) requires a sequential process: Youth $\rightarrow$ Employer $\rightarrow$ Parent/Guardian [2]. 
* **Scheduling mistakes are the #1 compliance risk for under-16s:** During the school year, 14- and 15-year-olds are strictly capped at 3 hours per day and 18 hours per week, between 7 a.m. and 7 p.m. [3]. A 30-minute off-duty meal break is mandatory after 5 continuous hours of work [3] [4].
* **"No hour caps" for 16–17-year-olds is not a free pass:** While Virginia imposes no state hour limits for 16- and 17-year-olds, compulsory school attendance laws and local curfews still apply [5]. In Richmond, a strict 11 p.m. to 5 a.m. curfew for minors under 18 requires employers to document "to/from work" travel to protect their teen workers [6] [7].
* **Hazard bans trump job titles across ages 14–17:** Minors under 18 are barred from hazardous work (e.g., explosives, power-driven saws, roofing, slaughtering) [8]. Minors under 16 face additional bans, including manufacturing, warehouses, and construction [8]. 
* **"17-year-olds driving" is a highly restricted exception:** Allowing a 17-year-old to drive on the job requires meeting nine strict conditions, including daylight-only driving, a 30-mile radius, and a ban on urgent or route deliveries [8].

## Who This Guide Serves and What’s at Stake

This guide is designed for teenagers seeking employment in Virginia and the businesses that hire them—particularly in retail, food service, recreation, agriculture, and entertainment. Missteps in youth employment compliance carry significant stakes. Employers face unannounced DOLI inspections and civil monetary penalties for failing to maintain proper records, proof of age, or signed Employment Certificates [1]. For teens and their parents, understanding these rules ensures a safe, legal, and balanced introduction to the workforce.

### Audience and Use Cases
* **Focus:** First-time jobs for 14–17-year-olds.
* **Key Scenarios:** School-year versus summer scheduling, navigating hazardous occupation bans, and managing late shifts in the Richmond metropolitan area.

## Minimum Age and Job-Type Eligibility by Age Group

Virginia law generally sets the minimum age for employment at 14, with very narrow exceptions for younger children [1]. As a minor ages, their eligibility expands, but safety-based task restrictions remain until age 18.

### At-a-Glance Eligibility by Age and Setting

| Age Group | General Work Allowed | Prohibited Core Areas | Notable Allowances | Key Citations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Under 14** | Exception-only (caddies, paper routes, domestic/farm work, parent-employed). | Inside commercial establishments; manufacturing; mining; hazardous roles. | Delivering newspapers; running outdoor errands; acting as a sports referee (12+). | [9] [10] |
| **14–15** | Most non-hazardous jobs (retail, food service, office clerical). | Manufacturing; warehouses; construction; processing in dry cleaning; hazardous roles. | Soda fountains; restaurant dining rooms; hotel food service; pool gatekeepers. | [8] [11] |
| **16–17** | All non-hazardous jobs with no state hour caps. | Explosives; logging; roofing; power-driven woodworking/metal machines. | 17-year-olds may drive under 9 strict conditions; volunteer firefighting (with training). | [5] [8] |
| **18+** | All occupations. | None (Adult rules apply). | N/A | [8] |

*Takeaway:* Employers must build an age-task matrix. A 14-year-old can work in a restaurant dining room but cannot work in a warehouse or manufacturing facility.

### Special Carve-outs and Exemptions
Virginia Code § 40.1-79.01 outlines specific exemptions where standard child labor laws (except extreme hazard bans) do not apply. These include:
* Domestic work performed in the child's own home for their parent [10].
* Occasional work around an employer's home (outside school hours) [10].
* Farm work for 12- and 13-year-olds with parental consent [10].
* Pages or clerks for the Virginia House of Delegates or Senate [10].
* Volunteer emergency medical services agency participation [10].
* Content creation (e.g., social media), provided the child is not exposed to serious hazards and is under direct adult supervision [10].

## Work Permits (Employment Certificates) — When, Who, and How

Work permits are the foundational compliance document for young teens. In Virginia, all 14- and 15-year-old workers must obtain an Employment Certificate *prior* to performing any work [1]. Minors aged 16 and 17 do not require an Employment Certificate [12].

### Electronic Process (VAeECS): 3-Step Flow

The primary method for obtaining a permit is the Virginia Electronic Employment Certificate System (VAeECS). The youth must have a firm job offer before beginning this process [1] [2].

| Step | Responsible Party | Action Required |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1. Youth Registration** | Teen | Registers online and receives a unique identifier number to give to the employer and parent [2]. |
| **2. Employer Registration** | Employer | Uses the youth's identifier to register intent to employ, detailing the occupation and hours [2] [13]. |
| **3. Legal Guardian Approval** | Parent/Guardian | Uses the identifier to provide legal consent for the employment [14] [2]. |

*Takeaway:* Once all three steps are complete, DOLI typically reviews the application within 24 hours. The approved certificate is issued electronically to the employer, but it **only becomes valid once signed by the youth worker** [1].

### Manual Process and Parental Consent
If the electronic system cannot be used, manual forms are available. This requires two documents submitted together:
1. **Permission to Employ Form:** Completed by the parent/guardian. **Crucially, this form must be notarized** by a Virginia-commissioned notary [15].
2. **Employer Intent to Employ Form:** Completed by the employer [1].
Approved manual certificates are mailed to the employer and must be signed by the youth [1].

### Employer Recordkeeping and Inspections
Employers are subject to unannounced DOLI inspections. During an audit, employers must produce:
* Signed Employment Certificates for all 14- and 15-year-olds [1].
* Proof of age documents (e.g., birth certificate, baptismal record) [1] [16].
* Time records showing beginning/ending times and meal periods for minors under 16, retained for **36 months** [1] [17].

## Hour Restrictions and Scheduling Rules

Scheduling 14- and 15-year-olds requires strict adherence to state hour caps, which fluctuate based on the school calendar. 

### Nonagricultural Hours for 14–15-Year-Olds

| Metric | School in Session | School Not in Session (Summer: June 1 – Labor Day) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Daily Maximum** | 3 hours on a school day | 8 hours on a non-school day |
| **Weekly Maximum** | 18 hours per week | 40 hours per week |
| **Time Window** | 7:00 a.m. to 7:00 p.m. | 7:00 a.m. to 9:00 p.m. |
| **School Hours** | Prohibited during school hours | N/A |

*Takeaway:* A mandatory 30-minute rest or meal period must be provided after five continuous hours of work. Periods of less than 30 minutes do not interrupt a continuous work period [3] [4].

### Agricultural Hours
For farms, gardens, and orchards, minors under 16 cannot work during school hours unless employed by their parent on a family-owned farm [3]. While daily/weekly hour caps are looser in agriculture, the 30-minute break rule after five continuous hours still strictly applies [3].

### Exceptions That Modify Hours
Certain roles exempt minors from standard hour limitations (though hazard bans still apply):
* **Newspaper Carriers:** Minors 12 and older can begin delivering newspapers at 4:00 a.m. [3] [18].
* **School Work-Training:** 14- and 15-year-olds enrolled in a regular school work-training program can work during school hours [3] [19].
* **Sports Attendants/Referees:** 14- and 15-year-olds performing sports-attending services at pro events, or 12+ year-olds refereeing for government/eleemosynary programs, are exempt from standard hour caps [3].

### 16–17-Year-Olds: No Hour Caps, But School and Curfew Apply
Teenagers aged 16 and 17 have no state-mandated restrictions on the number of hours they can work. However, they must comply with compulsory school attendance laws and local curfew ordinances, which practically limit their availability [5] [20] [21].

## Prohibited and Hazardous Occupations

Virginia strictly prohibits minors from working in environments deemed hazardous. These bans override any job title or parental consent (outside of specific family farm exemptions).

### Under-18 Hazard Bans
No child under 18 may be employed in:
* Manufacturing or storing explosives [8].
* Logging, sawmilling, or roofing operations [8].
* Excavation, demolition, or shipbreaking [8].
* Operating power-driven woodworking, metal-forming, bakery, or paper-products machines [8].
* Slaughtering or meatpacking [8].
* Occupations involving exposure to radioactive substances [8].

### Under-16 Additional Prohibitions vs. Specific Allowances

| Banned for Under-16s | Allowed for 14+ (Service/Clerical) |
| :--- | :--- |
| Manufacturing or mechanical establishments [8] | Office work of a clerical nature [8] |
| Warehouses and construction trades [8] | Restaurants, soda fountains, hotel food service [8] |
| Processing in laundries/dry cleaners [8] | Dry cleaning branch stores (no processing on site) [8] |
| Curb service restaurants and hotel room service [8] | Kitchen work, tray service, hall cleaning in hospitals [8] |
| Lifeguard at a beach [8] | Beach concessionaires (handling chairs/umbrellas) [8] |

### 17-Year-Old Driving Exception—Operational Guardrails
Driving on the job is generally banned for minors, but 17-year-olds may drive automobiles or trucks if **all nine** of the following conditions are met:
1. Vehicle is under 6,000 lbs gross weight and equipped with seatbelts [8].
2. Driving is restricted to daylight hours [8].
3. Teen has a valid license and no moving violations [8].
4. Teen has completed a state-approved driver education course [8].
5. **No** towing, route deliveries, urgent/time-sensitive deliveries, or transporting more than 3 passengers [8].
6. Maximum of two delivery trips away from the primary workplace per day [8].
7. Maximum of two passenger transport trips per day [8].
8. Driving takes place within a 30-mile radius of employment [8].
9. Driving is only occasional and incidental (max 1/3 of workday, max 20% of workweek) [8].

### Agriculture-Specific Hazard List for Under-16
On farms, minors under 16 are barred from operating tractors over 20 PTO horsepower, operating earthmoving equipment, forklifts, chain saws, or working in pens occupied by breeding bulls, boars, or stud horses [22].

## Local Overlay: Richmond Curfew and Real-World Scheduling

For employers and teens in the City of Richmond, local curfew ordinances act as a de facto scheduling constraint for late-night shifts.

### Curfew Rules and Exceptions

| Richmond Curfew Parameter | Details |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Curfew Hours** | 11:00 p.m. to 5:00 a.m. [6] [7] |
| **Affected Age Group** | Minors aged 17 and under [6] |
| **Work Exception** | Minors are exempt if they are working, or traveling directly to and from work and home [6] [7]. |
| **Other Exceptions** | Accompanied by parent/guardian; emergency errands; within 25 feet of home; school/civic activities [6] [7]. |

*Takeaway:* While 16- and 17-year-olds can legally work past 11 p.m., Richmond police actively enforce the curfew [6]. Employers should provide teen workers with employment verification letters stating their shift times to protect them from curfew violations while commuting home.

## Employer Compliance Systems and Checklists

To survive a DOLI inspection, employers must systematize their youth labor compliance.

### File and Record Controls

| Document Type | Requirement | Citation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Proof of Age** | Keep on file for all minors (e.g., birth certificate, baptismal record). | [13] [16] |
| **Employment Certificate** | Keep signed copies on file for all 14- and 15-year-olds. | [1] [11] |
| **Time Records** | Must show begin/end times and meal periods for under-16s. Retain for 36 months. | [1] [17] |
| **Theatrical Permits** | Required for minors in entertainment/performances. | [1] [23] |

*Takeaway:* Configure Human Capital Management (HCM) systems to block scheduling of 14- and 15-year-olds outside of the 7 a.m.–7 p.m. (or 9 p.m. summer) windows, and auto-flag shifts that lack a scheduled 30-minute break.

## Federal Law Interplay—Use the Stricter Standard

Virginia DOLI explicitly notes that the United States Department of Labor’s Wage and Hour Division enforces federal child labor laws alongside state laws. **Where both federal and state laws exist, the most stringent of the two laws applies** [1]. 
*Action:* Employers in food service must cross-check federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) rules, which place highly specific restrictions on 14- and 15-year-olds using deep fryers, baking equipment, and meat coolers.

## Special Cases: Entertainment, Sports, Newspapers, Solicitation

* **Theatrical Events:** Minors working in theatrical performances require a specific Theatrical Permit from DOLI, which involves an investigation into the performance environment [1] [23].
* **Solicitation:** Door-to-door solicitation is heavily regulated. No child under 16 may be employed by a solicitation permittee. 16- and 17-year-olds must work under immediate adult supervision, and the employer must hold a valid state permit and maintain strict records [24].

## Inferences and Unknowns

* **Inferences:** 
 * Virginia Administrative Code defines the extended summer hours (until 9 p.m.) as running from "June 1 through Labor Day" [3]. Employers should strictly revert to the 7 p.m. cutoff the day after Labor Day.
 * The manual "Permission to Employ" form explicitly requires notarization [15]. Employers utilizing the manual paper route must ensure parents have access to a notary, making the online VAeECS system vastly superior for speed of hiring.
* **Unknowns/Ambiguities:** 
 * While Richmond's curfew is clearly stated as 11 p.m. to 5 a.m. in the municipal code [6] [7], some public social media posts occasionally reference weekend variants (12 a.m. to 6 a.m.) [25]. Employers should rely on the strict 11 p.m. standard to ensure absolute compliance and safety.
