# Winning with Humility: Framing Thriving Families Prototypes for Real Richmond Impact

## Executive Summary
Richmond’s first-ever civic hackathon, Hack for RVA, is not a traditional technology sprint; it is a policy-driven event explicitly aligned with Mayor Danny Avula’s Mayoral Action Plan (MAP) [1]. For teams building solutions under Pillar Three: "Thriving Families," success requires framing weekend prototypes as credible, responsible, and impactful civic contributions without overclaiming. 

The most successful pitches will anchor their problem statements in Richmond's specific realities—such as the city's historically high eviction rates and concentrated child poverty [2] [3]. They will position their prototypes as "starting points" that validate core assumptions with real users, rather than finished products ready for citywide deployment [4]. Crucially, winning teams will frame ethical constraints—like data minimization, WCAG 2.1 AA accessibility, and human-in-the-loop AI—not as apologies for missing features, but as deliberate, compliance-ready design strengths [5] [6] [7]. By offering a realistic 30/60/90-day handoff plan to existing local partners, teams can prove their solution is built for real-world Richmond impact.

## Why This Framing Matters — Align to MAP, Earn Trust, and De-Risk Real Adoption

Richmond’s hackathon is about credible, MAP-linked contributions. Framing your weekend work around place, people, and policy beats flashy features.

### The Event’s DNA: People, Place, Purpose Over Pure Tech
Hack for RVA (March 27–29, 2026) challenges participants to prioritize "people, place, and purpose" over speed or technology alone [1]. The event features $10,000 in prizes, including a dedicated $1,000 award for each of the seven MAP pillars [1]. Mayor Avula has explicitly stated that the best solutions come from "listening, collaborating, and acting together" and must be "grounded in lived experience" [1]. Therefore, showing how your concept advances a specific MAP metric with local users is the primary scoring logic of the event.

## Richmond-Specific Problem Framing — Evictions, Poverty, and the Digital Divide

Cite local data, name neighborhoods, and tie family outcomes to housing and connectivity. Generic problem statements will fail; Richmond-specific realities will resonate.

### Data Snapshot: What Families Face Today
Family stability in Richmond is deeply intertwined with housing and digital access. Over one in four children in the city live in poverty [2]. Furthermore, housing instability is a hidden driver of family outcomes. 

| Issue | Local Evidence | Why it Matters for Thriving Families | Prototype Angles |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Evictions & Rent Burden** | Richmond had an 11.44% eviction rate in 2016 (#2 among large U.S. cities); 53% of Richmond renters are cost-burdened [3]. | Evictions disrupt school attendance, health, and job performance [3]. | Eviction Diversion Program (EDP) referral aids; arrears plan coaches; tenant rights navigators. |
| **Child Poverty** | Over one in four children in Richmond live in poverty [2]. | Undermines academic readiness and limits access to out-of-school activities [8] [2]. | Pre-K enrollment nudges; after-school seat finders; micro-support mapping. |
| **Digital Divide** | City Council declared gigabit internet a "public necessity" due to market failures excluding lower-income Black and Brown families [9]. | Access gaps block benefits, telehealth, and employment opportunities [9]. | Low-bandwidth design; SMS workflows; digital navigator handoffs. |

*Key Takeaway*: If your tool touches schooling, youth, or health, connect it to the eviction diversion and rent-burden realities that derail family stability. 

### Place-Based Context to Use in Pitches
Anchor your solution to specific places and policy pathways. The Richmond 300 Master Plan prioritizes reconnecting the historically Black neighborhood of Jackson Ward and redeveloping Gilpin Court [10]. Mentioning specific neighborhood anchors—like Richmond Public Schools (RPS) facilities, Main Street Station, or the Office of Community Wealth Building (OCWB) Empowerment Center on 25th Street [11] —demonstrates a deep understanding of the city's geography.

Make your demo speak the City’s language. Map your features directly to specific pillars, goals, and metrics.

## Framing Language Guide

Use precise, MAP-linked, place-based language and name your constraints as deliberate design choices.

### Problem Framing (Richmond-Specific)
* "In Richmond, housing instability is a child success issue. With 53% of renters cost-burdened and historically high eviction filings [3], families in Southside and the East End face constant disruption that shows up in RPS classrooms."
* "City Council has declared equitable gigabit access a public necessity [9]. Our concept assumes low-bandwidth and offline options so families without reliable internet can still benefit."

### Prototype Positioning (Starting Point, Not Product)
* "This is a proof-of-concept to validate whether a simple SMS and web flow can cut benefits application drop-off by 20% at one DSS site. Today’s demo uses synthetic data and three mocked screens."
* "What works today: the intake form and reminder flow. What’s simulated: agency system integration. What’s not included yet: multilingual support beyond Spanish and English."

### "What’s Next" Asks (Without Overpromising)
* "If DSS approves sandbox access and we complete a quick WCAG 2.1 AA check, we propose a 30-day alpha with 25 families at a Southside site, co-facilitated with OCWB. Success equals fewer abandoned applications and an 8/10 satisfaction score."

### Ethical Constraints as Strengths
* "We chose not to collect Personally Identifiable Information (PII) this weekend. That reduced risk and kept us focused on flow clarity. With a City MOU, we’ll add consented data later with human review."

## Starting Point vs. Finished Product

Overclaiming kills trust. Hackathons often conflate concept and capability; in government, that erodes buy-in and triggers compliance concerns. Swap certainty for conditional, metric-based language.

| Area | Say This (Starting Point) | Avoid This (Finished Product Claims) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Impact** | "Early signals suggest families complete forms faster in a sandbox test." | "We reduce processing times citywide." |
| **Scope** | "Validated with 5 parents and 3 front-line staff; next step is a 25-family alpha." | "Built for all Richmonders today." |
| **Data** | "Synthetic data only; no PII collected to ensure privacy." | "Fully integrated with DSS systems." |
| **AI** | "Prototype shows an explainable suggestion with human override." | "Our AI guarantees fair decisions." |
| **Accessibility** | "Designed toward WCAG 2.1 AA; needs formal screen reader testing." | "It’s 100% accessible." |

*Key Takeaway*: Separate demoed flows (working code), simulated data (mocked), and deferred features (out of scope).

## Ethical Constraints as Design Strengths

Privacy, accessibility, and transparency reduce risk and speed adoption. Frame these not as weekend limitations, but as enterprise-grade compliance features.

| Constraint | Why it Matters in Richmond | Our Design Choice |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **WCAG 2.1 AA (ADA Rule)** | A 2024 DOJ rule requires state/local government web and mobile apps to meet WCAG 2.1 AA within 2–3 years [7]. | High-contrast palette, keyboard navigation, alt-text, plain language; planned screen reader tests. |
| **Privacy-by-Design** | Eviction and benefits data are highly sensitive. 18F guidelines mandate mindfulness regarding PII [12]. | No PII in demo; synthetic datasets used; explicit consent screens built into the mock flow [5]. |
| **AI Bill of Rights** | Protects against algorithmic discrimination and requires notice, explanation, and human fallback options [6]. | Explanations provided for outputs + human fallback; no high-stakes automated decisions [6]. |
| **Digital Equity** | Council-mandated equity and Richmond 300 broadband focus on low-income areas [9] [10]. | SMS-first flows; lightweight page loads; offline instructions; digital navigator referrals. |

*Key Takeaway*: State clearly: "We met or planned toward WCAG 2.1 AA in these ways, and here is what we will test next with assistive tech users."

## Success and Failure Patterns

Learn from civic tech history: co-design beats assumptions, and small tests prevent big misses.

* **Failure Pattern**: The City of Boston spent $15,000–$25,000 each on public parklets that went unused because they were not tested with real users beforehand; the benches were uncomfortable and poorly placed [4].
* **Success Pattern**: San Francisco co-designed parklets with neighbors using temporary, low-cost prototypes adapted based on feedback, generating excitement and high adoption [4].
* **Principle**: Code for America emphasizes working *with* the community, integrating lived experience early, and treating impacted communities as valuable advisors [13]. 

## "What’s Next" Plans That Win

Judges reward feasible, near-term momentum. Offer a realistic path with clear decision points and a named City partner.

* **30 Days**: Conduct a co-design session with DSS intake staff and 10 families at one site. Establish an accessibility baseline and update flows. *Owner: DSS site lead.*
* **60 Days**: Launch a 25-family alpha test. Success metrics: -20% drop-off rate; +10 successful Eviction Diversion Program (EDP) referrals. *Owner: SCDHC program manager (for EDP concepts) or OCWB YES lead (for youth tools).*
* **90 Days**: Go/No-Go decision for a limited beta. Draft an MOU for data access and conduct a security and privacy review. *Owner: City IT + program lead.*
* **Sustainment**: Host the open-source repository under Code for RVA. Maintain a backlog for WCAG items and multilingual support, with quarterly community testing.

## Partner Ecosystem You Can Plug Into

Richmond already has "Thriving Families" infrastructure. Position your prototype as an intake, referral, or coaching support that connects to these existing programs.

* **Office of Community Wealth Building (OCWB)**: Runs Career Services, Youth Works RVA, barrier mitigation (childcare, transit), and the Richmond Resilience Initiative (guaranteed basic income) [11].
* **Eviction Diversion Program (EDP)**: Administered by Southside Community Development & Housing Corp. (SCDHC) and the Central Virginia Legal Aid Society (CVLAS). Aims to bring rent to a zero balance within three months with landlord participation [14] [15].
* **Richmond Public Schools (RPS)**: Focuses on after-school/athletics expansion and early childhood enrollment [8] [16].
* **Department of Social Services (DSS)**: Processes TANF, Refugee, Homeless, and Aging services (tracked under MAP Goal F) [8].

## Anticipate Skepticism

Preempt the "but what about..." questions from judges and city officials.

* **Data Access**: "No PII was used today. The next step is a sandbox MOU with scoped fields and strict retention limits."
* **Ownership**: "The OCWB Youth Engagement Services (YES) lead is the proposed product owner for the alpha; we will follow their prioritization."
* **Maintenance**: "Code for RVA will steward the repository. We propose monthly triage and a City-specific issue template."
* **Compliance**: "We built toward WCAG 2.1 AA standards [7]. We will run Trusted Tester checks pre-beta."
* **AI Risk**: "There are no automated decisions. Human review remains the primary mechanism, aligning with the AI Bill of Rights [6]."

## Inferences (Clearly Labeled)

* **Inference**: Eviction risk hotspots likely overlap with schools showing higher absenteeism. Connecting after-school enrollment support to EDP referrals could yield outsized gains in Pillar Three metrics. *Basis*: Research links evictions to school mobility and absenteeism [3]; Richmond has concentrated eviction filings [3] and Pillar Three targets out-of-school time goals [8].
* **Inference**: Low-bandwidth and SMS-first flows will be critical in Southside and East End pilots due to affordability and connectivity gaps. *Basis*: City Council's digital equity resolution [9] and the Richmond 300 plan's focus on expanding broadband to low-income areas [10].

## Unknowns

Be explicit about what you do not know yet to build credibility.

* Exact neighborhood-level current eviction filing rates by census tract (latest disaggregated data for 2026 is not pulled here).
* Current DSS system integration constraints, consent requirements, and technical interfaces (APIs/sandboxes).
* Affordable Connectivity Program (ACP) enrollment pipeline status post-federal program changes, and local digital navigator capacity.
* RPS site-level aftercare capacity and enrollment processes by individual school.
* City security review timelines and required artifacts for launching pilots.
