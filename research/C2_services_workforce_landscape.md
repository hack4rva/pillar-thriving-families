# Richmond Youth Employment: Who Serves Whom, How They Coordinate, and Where to Build Next

## Executive Summary

Richmond’s youth employment ecosystem is anchored by the Capital Region Workforce Partnership (CRWP) and City-run Youth Engagement Services (YES), supplemented by selective nonprofit and disability-focused pathways. While the regional workforce board demonstrates strong capacity and improving youth placement rates, the system suffers from fragmented in-school coverage, seasonal churn, and a lack of a unified cross-agency referral platform. City-funded summer jobs serve as a critical entry point, but the handoff to year-round employment or training is informal. To maximize impact, stakeholders must establish shared referral protocols, embed workforce navigators directly into Richmond Public Schools (RPS), and co-enroll eligible youth across overlapping programs to pool resources and sustain momentum.

## Landscape Overview by Organization Type

Richmond’s youth employment landscape features a mix of regional public workforce systems, city-funded initiatives, specialized disability programs, and community nonprofits. 

### Regional Workforce Board: Capital Region Workforce Partnership (CRWP)
The CRWP manages federal WIOA funds for the region, serving over 28,000 jobseekers in FY24, a 12% increase from the prior year [1]. The partnership has seen youth job placement rates improve to 81% in FY24 [1]. Out-of-school youth (OSY) services are operated by Ross Employment Solutions, while smaller in-school youth (ISY) contracts are managed by Peace of Mind and Charles City County [2] [1]. 

### City-Funded Programs: Office of Community Wealth Building (OCWB)
The OCWB operates Youth Engagement Services (YES), which runs Youth Works RVA, an eight-week summer workforce development program for youth aged 14–24 [3] [4]. The program offers multiple tracks, including work-based learning, entrepreneurship, and leadership development [3]. For older youth (18–24) who are out of school or unemployed, the YES Forward program provides occupational training in fields like healthcare, skilled trades, and IT [3].

### Disability Pathways: Project SEARCH
Project SEARCH, facilitated locally by VCU's Rehabilitation Research and Training Center (VCU-RRTC), provides a one-year transition program for young adults with disabilities aged 18 to 22 [5] [6]. The program features total immersion in an employer agency, such as Bon Secours hospitals, through three internships [5] [6]. Statewide, the program boasts an 85% success rate in helping students find meaningful jobs [5].

### Community Nonprofits and Tech Training
The Boys & Girls Clubs of Metro Richmond (BGCMR) operates a Teen & Community Center for youth in grades 9–12, providing no-cost memberships and workforce readiness programming like the Ready2Win initiative [7] [8] [9]. Additionally, NPower offers a free, virtual Tech Fundamentals program providing up to 20 weeks of IT training [10].

## Coordination Assessment

Coordination within the public workforce system is structured, but inter-agency youth referrals rely heavily on informal relationships rather than a shared intake backbone.

### One-Stop Operations and Referrals
Equus serves as the region's "One Stop Operator," ensuring that workforce centers and partners operate collaboratively with high-quality standards [2] [1]. However, there is no public evidence of a unified cross-system referral platform connecting City YES programs, RPS, VCW, and major nonprofits like BGCMR.

### Employer Engagement
Employer engagement is currently parallel but siloed. YES secures citywide partners for summer placements [3], CRWP runs OJT/WEX for WIOA participants [2], and Project SEARCH cultivates healthcare partners [6].
