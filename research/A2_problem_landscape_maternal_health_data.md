## Global Mortality Overview

The global burden of maternal mortality remains a critical public health challenge, despite decades of targeted interventions. In 2023, an estimated 260,000 maternal deaths occurred worldwide, which equates to over 700 women dying every day from preventable causes related to pregnancy and childbirth [1] [2] [3]. 

While the global maternal mortality ratio (MMR) has seen a 40% reduction since 2000—dropping from 328 to 197 deaths per 100,000 live births—the distribution of these deaths is highly unequal [2] [4]. Approximately 92% of all maternal deaths occur in low- and lower-middle-income countries [1]. Conversely, high-income countries report significantly lower overall rates, though disparities exist within these populations. For example, in the United States, the 2023 maternal mortality rate showed stark age-based differences: 12.5 deaths per 100,000 live births for women under 25, 18.1 for ages 25–39, and a concerning 59.8 for women aged 40 and older [7].

## Data Landscape

### Civil Registration & Vital Statistics (CRVS)

Civil Registration and Vital Statistics (CRVS) systems are the foundational bedrock for tracking maternal mortality, yet they suffer from severe coverage gaps. Globally, nearly 40% of deaths remain unregistered [5]. The situation is particularly dire in low-income countries, where only 8% of reported deaths have documented causes [5]. Even when CRVS systems are operational, modeling estimates suggest that only 64.4% of maternal deaths are correctly identified within these systems [8]. This systemic under-reporting skews mortality ratios and masks local hotspots, making it difficult to allocate resources effectively.

### Routine Health Information Systems (RHIS) – DHIS2

The District Health Information Software 2 (DHIS2) serves as the national health information system in many low- and middle-income countries, collecting routine population data for various performance indicators [9]. However, its effectiveness is frequently compromised by operational and infrastructural barriers.

| Challenge Area | Specific Barriers Identified | Impact on Maternal Health Data |
|---|---|---|
| **Infrastructure & Systems** | Server breakdowns, system errors, and infrastructural challenges [6]. | Prevents timely data entry and access, leading to incomplete maternal health records. |
| **Human Resources & Access** | Lack of access rights to key cadres [6]. | Restricts the ability of frontline maternal health workers to input or utilize data. |
| **Data Quality & Utilization** | Broadly fragmented systems with non-standardized data and limited evidence of data being used to inform decisions [10]. | Reduces trust in the data, meaning critical maternal health decisions are made without empirical backing. |

### Survey & Surveillance Systems

To bridge the gaps left by CRVS and RHIS, specialized surveillance systems are utilized. The Maternal Death Surveillance and Response (MDSR) framework provides technical guidance to health care professionals and managers to improve maternal health by identifying and reviewing maternal deaths [11]. In higher-resource settings, national health statistics centers (such as the CDC's NCHS) provide provisional and finalized reports that allow for granular, age-stratified analysis of maternal mortality trends [7].

## Core Drivers of Data Gaps

The persistent gaps in maternal health data are driven by a combination of institutional, technological, and socio-economic factors:

* **Fragmented Governance:** Health information systems are often institutionally fragmented, leading to siloed data that cannot be easily cross-referenced or validated [10].
* **Technological Constraints:** Barriers to the adoption and effective use of digital systems like DHIS2 include severe infrastructural challenges and system errors [6].
* **Human Resource Limitations:** Weak human resources capacity and a lack of access rights for key personnel hinder the accurate collection and utilization of data [6] [10].
* **Socio-Cultural Barriers:** Uneven access to affordable, high-quality health care impedes improvements in maternal survival and affects the likelihood of maternal complications being formally recorded within the health system [12].

## Opportunities & Strategic Levers

Addressing the maternal health data landscape requires targeted interventions across multiple systemic levels. 

| Strategic Lever | Implementation Focus | Expected Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **CRVS Strengthening** | Implement technical guidance to improve the identification of maternal deaths within civil registration systems [11] [13]. | Higher percentage of correctly identified maternal deaths (currently estimated at 64.4%) [8]. |
| **DHIS2 Optimization** | Resolve infrastructural bottlenecks, expand access rights to relevant health cadres, and standardize data inputs [6] [10]. | Improved routine data quality and increased utilization for local health decision-making. |
| **Integrated Surveillance** | Expand Maternal Death Surveillance and Response (MDSR) initiatives to link directly with national health information systems [11]. | Actionable, real-time insights that prevent future maternal deaths through immediate localized responses. |
| **Age-Stratified Monitoring** | Adopt age-specific mortality tracking globally, mirroring the detailed reporting seen in U.S. CDC data [7]. | Better identification of high-risk demographics (e.g., older mothers) to tailor clinical guidelines. |

## Recommendations for Stakeholders

1. **Policy Makers:** Mandate the integration of CRVS and routine health information systems (like DHIS2) to reduce institutional fragmentation and improve data standardization [10].
2. **Health Facility Managers:** Ensure that key health cadres are granted appropriate access rights to DHIS2 to facilitate accurate and timely data entry [6].
3. **International Donors:** Prioritize funding for infrastructural upgrades (e.g., server stability, internet access) to support the reliable operation of digital health information systems in low-income settings [6].
4. **Public Health Officials:** Utilize technical guidance on Maternal Death Surveillance and Response (MDSR) to transform raw mortality data into actionable, preventative health measures [11].
