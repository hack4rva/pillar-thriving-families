# Richmond Maternal Health Data Users — Who Publishes What, Who Needs What, and Where a Shared Tool Fits Now

## Executive Summary
Richmond’s maternal health ecosystem features robust state-level data dashboards and highly active community-based organizations, yet significant local disparities persist. While Virginia's overall preterm birth rate is 10.1%, Richmond City receives an "F" grade from the March of Dimes with a 12.5% preterm birth rate [1]. Similarly, Richmond City's infant mortality rate of 9.6 per 1,000 live births starkly contrasts with the statewide rate of 6.0 per 1,000 [2]. 

Despite high rates of early prenatal care initiation (96.5%), Richmond mothers face significant support gaps, including inadequate care (11.6%), pre-pregnancy depression (16.9%), and low WIC enrollment (27.1%) [3]. Community-based providers like Urban Baby Beginnings and Birth in Color RVA are already tracking critical outcomes such as mental health trends, rehospitalizations, and doula interventions [4] [5]. To bend the curve on Richmond's maternal health crisis, a shared data coordination tool must bridge the gap between clinical prenatal care starts and holistic, community-supported outcomes. By anchoring this tool with the Richmond and Henrico Health Districts (RHHD) and primary community hubs, stakeholders can align program targeting, automate referral follow-ups, and generate unified advocacy metrics.

## Scope and Value Proposition
A shared maternal health data coordination tool in Richmond will transform how local organizations track, fund, and deliver care. Currently, the Virginia Department of Health (VDH) provides comprehensive state and district-level dashboards [6] [7], but community organizations and hospital systems often struggle to access real-time, neighborhood-level outcome data for their specific interventions. By mapping maternal health data users and their data flows, this report identifies where coordination can move the region's focus from simply tracking "early prenatal care started" to ensuring "adequate, supported, and equitable birth outcomes."

## Current Outcomes Picture
Richmond City’s preterm and infant mortality burdens significantly outpace Virginia’s state averages, revealing a critical need for localized data tracking and intervention.

### City vs. State Disparities
State-level improvements obscure severe local risks. The 2025 March of Dimes Report Card grades Virginia with a "C-" for its 10.1% preterm birth rate, but Richmond City earns an "F" with a 12.5% rate [1]. Mortality data echoes this disparity: the Children's Hospital of Richmond (CHoR) 2025 Community Health Needs Assessment (CHNA) reports an infant mortality rate of 9.6 per 1,000 live births in Richmond City, compared to the statewide rate of 6.0 per 1,000 [2]. 

### Richmond PRAMS Fast Facts Reveal Support Gaps
Clinical access does not guarantee adequate care. According to the 2022 Pregnancy Risk Assessment Monitoring System (PRAMS) data for the Richmond City Health District (based on 348 local respondents), 96.5% of mothers began prenatal care in the first trimester [3]. However, underlying vulnerabilities remain high:
* **11.6%** received inadequate prenatal care according to the Kotelchuck index [3].
* **27.1%** were enrolled in WIC during pregnancy [3].
* **16.9%** reported pre-pregnancy depression [3].
* **9.6%** had no insurance to pay for prenatal care [3].
* **35.1%** were tested for HIV during pregnancy [3].

### Neighborhood Targeting Need
Granular data is essential for deploying resources effectively. The CHoR CHNA highlights that 11.7% of Richmond City births are preterm, 11.0% are low birthweight, and 4.5% receive late or no prenatal care [2]. A shared coordination tool must utilize these locality-level metrics to define tract-level priorities for doula deployment and maternity navigation.

**Key Takeaways:** The ecosystem is bifurcated between organizations that provide direct, trackable interventions (RHHD, UBB, Birth in Color) and organizations that require aggregated data for needs assessments and policy (Hospitals, United Way, March of Dimes). The coordination tool must serve the operational needs of the former to automatically generate the reporting needs of the latter.

## Primary vs. Secondary User Strategy
To ensure adoption, the tool must be anchored where services meet outcomes, subsequently connecting to amplifiers and funders.

### Phase 1: Primary Users
The initial rollout should focus on organizations actively managing patient panels and tracking interventions. RHHD Population Health and Maternity Navigation will serve as the anchor, given their existing use of data teams to analyze birth outcomes and their utilization of the Unite Us platform [10]. Urban Baby Beginnings, Birth in Color, and Nurture RVA must be onboarded simultaneously to capture doula interventions, mental health tracking, and lactation support metrics [4] [5] [14]. Alignment with the Virginia Neonatal Perinatal Collaborative (VNPC) will ensure these community metrics map to state quality standards [15].

### Phase 2: Secondary Users
Once primary data flows are established, secondary users can be granted read-only or aggregate-level access. CHoR and Bon Secours will use the tool to pull de-identified data for their CHNAs [18] [2]. United Way and March of Dimes can utilize the aggregated data to update their community indicator reports [1] [19]. Planned Parenthood, operating primarily as a family planning and abortion care provider at its Church Hill location [21] [22], should be integrated as a referral gateway to track successful connections to prenatal care and navigation services.

### Data Exchange Approach
The tool should feature standard exports formatted for VDH dashboards, de-identified cohort generation for hospital CHNAs, and closed-loop referral analytics for gateway clinics like Planned Parenthood.

## Shared Metrics and Data Standards
To make data both authoritative and actionable, the tool must mirror state constructs while adding community-program outcomes.

### Core Clinical Indicators
The tool's data model must align with VDH definitions for Preterm Birth, Low Birthweight, Severe Maternal Morbidity (SMM), and Maternal Mortality [6] [8]. Furthermore, it should track PRAMS-aligned metrics at the individual level, including prenatal care adequacy (Kotelchuck index), WIC uptake, depression screening, and HIV testing [3]. 

### Community Outcomes
Beyond clinical metrics, the tool must standardize a lightweight indicator set for community interventions. This includes tracking doula engagement, postpartum rehospitalizations (currently tracked by UBB) [4], breastfeeding initiation and duration (aligned with Nurture RVA) [14], and closed-loop referral success rates.

### Data Governance
Implementation requires strict Data Use Agreements (MOUs), PHI minimization, role-based access controls, and suppression rules that align with VDH standards (e.g., suppressing data for very small sample sizes to protect privacy) [2].

## Priority Use Cases
The shared data tool will power three primary strategic functions for Richmond organizations.

### Targeted Outreach
By overlaying PeriStats and CHoR baseline data (e.g., Richmond's 12.5% preterm rate) [1] [2] with real-time community data, RHHD and community hubs can deploy doulas and maternity navigators directly to high-risk census tracts, shifting from reactive to proactive care.

### Grant and Advocacy Packaging
The tool will auto-generate advocacy briefs combining authoritative baselines (VDH/PeriStats) with local partner outcomes. This allows organizations like Birth in Color to easily pull impact metrics for events like Black Maternal Health Week or for city budget funding requests [5].

### Closed-Loop Referrals
Integrating the tool with existing platforms like Unite Us (used by RHHD) [10] and gateway clinics like Planned Parenthood [22] will allow the network to monitor time-to-care, insurance resolution bottlenecks, and ultimate prenatal care adequacy.

## Successes to Scale and Cautions to Heed

### Successes to Scale
* **Survey-to-Learning Pipelines:** Nurture RVA successfully translated #RVAbreastfeeds survey data into actionable community webinars [14].
* **Navigation Volume:** RHHD's Maternity Navigation successfully served 295 clients in 2024 and established workflows for Medicaid application assistance and car seat distribution [10].
* **Funded Doula Models:** Birth in Color has proven the funding model for community doulas, securing $150K from RCHD and $33K from Health Equity Funding for local projects [5].

### Cautions to Heed
* **The State Average Trap:** Relying on Virginia's overall 10.1% preterm birth rate masks Richmond City's severe 12.5% rate [1]. All reporting must require city- and tract-level breakouts.
* **The "Early PNC" Illusion:** While 96.5% of Richmond mothers start care early, 11.6% still receive inadequate care [3]. Metrics must focus on adequacy, not just initiation.
* **Dated Indicators:** Some regional indicator reports (e.g., United Way) rely on older data [19] [20], limiting their current advocacy power. The tool must provide real-time updates.

## Risks, Inferences, and Unknowns
To ensure the tool's success, several gaps in the current public data landscape must be addressed during the implementation phase.

### Unknowns
* **FIMR Outputs:** Public summaries or specific findings from the Richmond Fetal and Infant Mortality Review (FIMR) are not readily accessible in the current data.
* **Local Home Visiting Feeds:** Specific data feeds and site-level outcomes for Richmond-based Nurse-Family Partnership or Resource Mothers programs are unknown.
* **Referral Volumes:** The exact volume of prenatal referrals originating from Planned Parenthood's Richmond health center is undocumented [22].
* **Hospital Data Sharing:** The specific terms under which CHoR and Bon Secours will share internal data versus pulling external data remain undefined.

### Inferences (Clearly Labeled)
* *Inference:* CHIP of Virginia and Nurse-Family Partnership are active in the Greater Richmond area via Families Forward Virginia partners, and their outcomes (e.g., 40% work increase, $1,163 savings per birth) [23] [24] can be localized if MOUs are established.
* *Inference:* The Richmond FIMR operates under the standard VDH model and possesses qualitative case data that could highly enrich the coordination tool's risk modeling.

### Mitigations
These unknowns will be mitigated by establishing pilot data-sharing agreements (MOUs) in the first 30 days, utilizing privacy-by-design architecture, and publishing a living "data source registry" within the application so all users understand data provenance.

## Implementation Roadmap
A phased, 90-day rollout will establish the tool's value without overwhelming partner capacity.

### Day 0–30: Foundation and Baselines
Build the data model to mirror VDH, PRAMS, and SMM definitions [6] [3] [8]. Onboard RHHD Population Health and one primary CBO (Urban Baby Beginnings or Birth in Color) [10] [4] [5]. Import historical baselines from PeriStats and the CHoR CHNA [1] [2].

### Day 31–60: Integration and Tracking
Establish referral integrations with Unite Us and Planned Parenthood [10] [22]. Begin active tracking of WIC enrollment, depression screening, and prenatal care adequacy [3]. Generate the first automated advocacy brief template for use in grant writing.

### Day 61–90: Expansion and Publication
Add CHoR and Bon Secours as secondary, read-only users to support their CHNA cycles [18] [2]. Execute MOUs with Families Forward/CHIP for home visiting data [23]. Publish the first quarterly city/tract-level maternal health dashboard for public and funder visibility.
